Study Guide — Section 1 · EDU-302

Delivery Consultant Overview

The first stop in the EDU-302 learning path — orient yourself to the Zscaler for Users curriculum, recap what EDU-200 and EDU-202 covered, and preview the 11 courses ahead.

Three learning paths. One platform mastery.

Administrator (EDU-200) taught you what Zscaler is. Engineer (EDU-202) taught you how to operate it. Delivery Consultant (EDU-302) teaches you how to design, deploy, and own customer outcomes — the final step before ZDDC certification.

💭THINK FIRST · COURSE OVERVIEW

Zscaler's certification track has three role-based paths — Administrator, Engineer, and Delivery Consultant. Each is built on the last. Before you read this overview, think about why that sequence exists and what role-shift each path represents.

  • Why might Zscaler structure the curriculum as Administrator → Engineer → Delivery Consultant, instead of one combined course? What does each role contribute that the next role builds on?
  • The Delivery Consultant ends with a Capstone Project — not just a multiple-choice exam. What does that tell you about how this role is assessed compared to the previous two?
  • If you skipped EDU-200 and EDU-202 and started straight at EDU-302, what specifically would you struggle with — and why does Zscaler call the prior paths "highly recommended" rather than mandatory?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
Each path maps to a real customer-facing role. Administrator (EDU-200) owns the day-to-day operation of a tenant — policies, users, logs. Engineer (EDU-202) owns design and integration — building the architecture, integrating IdPs, troubleshooting at the platform level. Delivery Consultant (EDU-302) owns the customer-success outcome — translating a customer's business goals into a Zscaler deployment that actually lands. You can't design a deployment well without first knowing the operational primitives.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
The Capstone Project signals that this is an applied role — you can't fake it by memorising answers. You have to scope, design, and present a deployment end-to-end. That's exactly what a consultant does in front of a customer, so the assessment mirrors the job. The earlier paths test knowledge; this one tests judgement.
Q3 — MODEL ANSWER
EDU-302 spends very little time defining what ZIA, ZPA, ZDX, and ZIdentity are — it assumes you already know. Without EDU-200/EDU-202, every Access Control or Cyberthreat lesson would land on shaky ground, and the Capstone would be unmanageable. "Highly recommended" gives experienced practitioners a path to skip ahead, but the realistic expectation is that you've built the prior foundation.
COURSE OVERVIEW

Welcome to Zscaler for Users — Delivery Consultant

This is Course 1 of 11 in the Zscaler for Users — Delivery Consultant (EDU-302) learning path, the third major path inside the Zscaler Cyber Academy. The Cyber Academy organises training into role-based platform paths (Administrator, Engineer, Delivery Consultant — with Architect "coming soon") plus specialty domain courses (Data Protection, Cyberthreat Protection, Zero Trust Networking, Risk Management) and the Zscaler Digital Experience operational track.

Where EDU-302 sits: Foundation level = ZCCA (Zero Trust Certified Associate). Role-based paths = EDU-200 (Administrator) → EDU-202 (Engineer) → EDU-302 (Delivery Consultant). Specialty courses sit underneath the role paths and dive deep into one solution area at a time.

In this overview, you'll briefly recap what EDU-200 and EDU-202 covered, then preview the 11 courses inside EDU-302 itself — so you arrive at each subsequent course knowing exactly how the pieces connect.

💭THINK FIRST · RECAP EDU-200 ADMINISTRATOR

EDU-200 covered ten courses — from the Zero Trust Exchange platform overview all the way to Zero Trust Automation. Before reading the recap, see if you can predict which ten domains a Zscaler administrator must know cold. The list itself tells you what an administrator owns day-to-day.

  • Of the ten EDU-200 courses, which three do you think show up most frequently in real day-to-day administrator work — and which one is most easily forgotten between exams?
  • EDU-200 starts with the Zero Trust Exchange overview and ends with Zero Trust Automation. Why is automation positioned last in the curriculum — what does an administrator need to understand first before automating anything?
  • Identity Services (SAML, SCIM, IdPs, ZIdentity) sits very early in the path. What does that placement signal about how Zscaler thinks about identity in the platform architecture?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
Identity Services, Access Control, and Cyberthreat Protection drive the bulk of admin tickets — login problems, blocked applications, policy exceptions. The most easily forgotten is usually Zero Trust Automation, because most admins never write an API call until they hit a scale problem; it's the last to be exercised, so the first to be forgotten.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
You can't automate what you don't understand. Automation is sequenced last because every prior course teaches the policies, objects, and workflows that the API endpoints expose. Trying to automate before you understand the manual workflow produces broken scripts and inconsistent state across tenants.
Q3 — MODEL ANSWER
Identity is the spine of the Zero Trust Exchange — without an authenticated user/device context, every downstream policy (access control, DLP, cyberthreat) loses its anchor. Teaching identity early signals that every later policy decision references "who is this and what posture are they in" before it asks "what should they reach". Identity first is a design principle, not just curriculum ordering.

💭THINK FIRST · RECAP EDU-202 ENGINEER

EDU-202 has eleven courses — one more than EDU-200. The added course at the front is "Zscaler for Users — Engineer Overview". Beyond that, the topics map roughly one-to-one with EDU-200 but go deeper — multi-tenant architecture, step-up auth, SD-WAN integration, AppProtection. Think about what the word "deeper" actually means at this level.

  • An EDU-200 administrator can configure SAML SSO. An EDU-202 engineer can do the same — but additionally configures step-up authentication and external identity providers. What real-world scenario forces an organisation from "basic SAML" up to "step-up + multiple IdPs"?
  • EDU-202 introduces "Sub-clouds and China Premium Access" as architecture features. What customer need does each of these solve — and why are they Engineer-level topics rather than Administrator-level?
  • The Engineer path covers "Workflow Automation" inside Data Protection. How is that different from the API-level Zero Trust Automation at the end of EDU-200?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
Scenarios that force step-up auth + multiple IdPs include: mergers and acquisitions (two IdPs need to coexist while you migrate), contractor populations on a separate directory, compliance regimes requiring stronger auth before reaching sensitive applications, and organisations that need different MFA strengths for different application sensitivities. Administrators inherit those configurations and operate them; engineers design and connect them in the first place.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
Sub-clouds let global enterprises pin their tenant to a specific regulatory region (e.g. EU-only data residency) — an architectural decision, not a daily setting. China Premium Access is the equivalent for the unique connectivity and compliance challenges of operating inside the Great Firewall. Both are Engineer-level because they're chosen up-front during architecture design, are operationally expensive to change later, and touch contracting and legal — not just admin UI.
Q3 — MODEL ANSWER
Zero Trust Automation (EDU-200 closing course) is the API and OneAPI framework — the programmable surface. Workflow Automation in EDU-202 is the human-process orchestration within Data Protection — e.g. an alert triggers a ticket, an admin reviews and approves, a remediation runs. One is "how do machines call the platform"; the other is "how do humans + the platform coordinate around an incident".
RECAP · EDU-202 ENGINEER

What EDU-202 Covered

EDU-202 is eleven courses. It's the architectural and integration layer — the engineer can stand up a tenant from scratch, integrate it with the customer's identity, network, SIEM, and SD-WAN, and tune the platform's behaviour. Below are the additional or deeper topics on top of EDU-200's foundation.

1. Zscaler for Users — Engineer Overview
Recap of EDU-200 plus what Zscaler Experience Center adds — simplifying zero trust administration.
2. Zscaler Architecture
Foundational multi-tenant architecture at global scale. Advanced capabilities including Sub-clouds and China Premium Access, plus the functionality for programmatic access, control, and configuration of the ZTE.
3. Identity Services (deeper)
SAML + OIDC workflows, step-up authentication, sessions and methods, external IdPs, admin SSO and password policy.
4. Connectivity Services (deeper)
Configuring advanced tunnelling modes and traffic forwarding in Zscaler Client Connector. Role of Zscaler Branch Connector in connecting servers and IoT/OT devices. Troubleshooting blockage. Zscaler Cloud Connector for secure access to cloud workloads and Browser Access for clientless access. SD-WAN integration.
5. Platform Services (deeper)
Private Service Edges in ZIA and ZPA. SSL Inspection and Global Control Policies. Differentiating on-premise vs cloud-based SIEM. Authentication and Idle Connection Timeout policies in ZPA. ZPA Anchoring to enable Private Service Edges + Application Segments + Cloud-to-Cloud Log Streaming.
6. Access Control Services (deeper)
Proxy-based firewall architecture. DNS protection for end-users, machines, and all endpoint types. Configuring Private Application Access. Steps for deploying App Connectors.
7. Cyberthreat Protection Services (deeper)
AI Instant Verdict, Quarantine, Deception, Zscaler ITDR, Browser Isolation. Advanced Threat Protection — Cloud Sandbox policies, IPS and Custom IPS Signatures, Deception Campaign, AppProtection in ZPA, Browser Isolation.
8. Data Protection Services (deeper)
Advanced data protection services — full data channel coverage. Managing data protection incidents with Zscaler's administrator portal. Workflow Automation for alerting administrators of DLP violations.
9. Risk Management (deeper)
Optimising the Zscaler Risk Management platform — proactive threat detection and mitigation. Advanced features of Risk360. Zscaler Data Fabric for actionable insights. ZUVM for org-wide vulnerability assessment. Strategic EASM. ITDR for identity-based threat detection. Breach Predictor.
10. Zscaler Digital Experience (deeper)
Purpose, core components, and deployment methodology of ZDX. ZDX traffic forwarding, monitoring policies, application probes, alerts. Admin Portal — monitor and analyse issues, troubleshoot. Operationalising ZDX — staged rollout, access control, cloud connectivity. ZDX metrics + AI-driven insights to reduce ticket escalations.
11. Zscaler Zero Trust Automation (deeper)
Architecture and workflow of OneAPI. Configuration of OneAPI using Postman. Authentication and access control via ZIdentity. Integrating OneAPI with the Postman REST API client. Common automation use cases.

💭THINK FIRST · EDU-302 LEARNING PATH

The EDU-302 path has 11 eLearning courses, a hands-on Lab, and a Capstone Project — and the Lab + Capstone are bundled into a single 12-hour session. Before reading on, think about why the Lab and Capstone are deliberately combined rather than separated, and what that says about how Delivery Consultants are expected to work.

  • The Lab and Capstone share one 12-hour session. What's the design intent — and what would change if they were split into two separate sessions on different days?
  • Each EDU-302 course ends with a knowledge check before the next one unlocks. Why is this gating useful for a consulting-track path specifically (compared to a fast-read overview path)?
  • Course 2 is "Zscaler Deployment Methodology". Why is methodology positioned that early — before any specific service like Identity or Connectivity?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
Real consulting engagements aren't broken into clean stages — labs (configuration) and capstones (design, presentation, decisions) happen interleaved with the customer in the room. Bundling them into a single 12-hour session mirrors a compressed customer workshop: you don't finish all the config and then start the design — you design while you configure and adjust mid-stream. Splitting them would make it feel like exams instead of a real engagement.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
A consultant has to assess a learner's understanding before they bring weak knowledge into a customer conversation. Gating with a knowledge check forces self-correction at each step, so the Capstone isn't the first time a misconception surfaces. For a fast-read overview path it'd just feel like friction; for a path culminating in real customer delivery, the friction is the point.
Q3 — MODEL ANSWER
Without a methodology, the rest of the path is just nine deep dives into services with no scaffold to fit them together. Methodology = how you scope, design, deploy, validate, and handover. It's positioned at Course 2 so every subsequent service course can be read through the lens of "where does this fit in a real deployment phase?". Methodology is the connective tissue of the whole path.

💭THINK FIRST · LEARNING OUTCOMES

Ten outcomes describe what you'll be able to do at the end of EDU-302. Action verbs matter: "Explain", "Implement", "Define", "Apply", "Discuss", "Illustrate". Before reading the list, think about which verbs are hardest to assess — and which you'd find easiest to fake in a customer conversation.

  • "Explain" and "Discuss" appear repeatedly across the ten outcomes. What's the practical difference between those two verbs in a consulting context? When would a customer notice the difference?
  • Outcome 5 is "Define granular controls, advanced deployment strategies, and real-world use cases". Why is "Define" the right verb here, rather than "Implement" — and what would change if it said "Implement"?
  • If you could only deeply master three of the ten outcomes before sitting the Capstone, which three would give you the most coverage of the others? Justify your pick.
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
"Explain" means you can tell someone how something works in a structured, accurate way. "Discuss" means you can compare options, surface tradeoffs, and engage the customer's pushback — closer to a dialogue than a monologue. Customers notice the difference the moment they ask "but what about my edge case?" — an "explainer" stalls; a "discusser" reframes and brings the conversation forward.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
"Define" forces clarity about what each control is and when it applies — the consultant's job is to draw boundaries, not to push buttons. If the verb were "Implement", the outcome would shrink to a hands-keyboard task; the strategic work of choosing between strategies and arguing for one in a customer workshop would disappear. The Delivery Consultant role is judgement-led; the verb signals that.
Q3 — MODEL ANSWER
Outcomes 1 (Design Methodology), 2 (Identity authentication + provisioning), and 10 (Zero Trust Automation framework + Delivery Consultant role) cover the widest surface. Methodology is the structural frame every other outcome plugs into. Identity is the gatekeeper every downstream service references. Outcome 10 maps directly to the Delivery Consultant role itself and ties automation into deployment. Master those three and you have the spine — the other seven are flesh on bones you already understand.

Analogy
The ten outcomes are the graduation rubric for the residency. Each one is a station on the practical exam — you don't pass by being amazing at one and weak at the others. The Capstone Project is the supervised case where each of the ten gets quietly assessed by someone watching how you handle the patient — the customer — in front of you.
