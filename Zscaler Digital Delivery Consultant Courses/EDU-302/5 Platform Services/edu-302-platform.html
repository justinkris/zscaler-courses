Study Guide — Section 5 · EDU-302

Platform Services

Policy design, SSL inspection at scale, log streaming + SIEM choice, ZPA timeout policies, specialised policies (Browser Access, Double Encryption, Machine Authentication), and Private Service Edge deployment — the layer that turns Zscaler from "connected" to "behaving exactly as the customer expects".

Policy is where the platform becomes the customer's.

Connectivity gets traffic to Zscaler. Identity proves who's behind the traffic. Platform Services is where the customer's actual security intent gets encoded — SSL inspection scope, log retention strategy, timeout tolerances, specialised access for edge cases. Get this layer wrong and the rest of the deployment doesn't matter.

💭THINK FIRST · OVERVIEW

Platform Services covers four distinct topics — Policy Types, SSL Logging & Analytics, Timeout Policies, Specialised Policies — that look unrelated on the surface. Before reading on, think about what connects them: each one encodes a different kind of customer intent into the platform.

  • SSL inspection, log retention, timeout values, and specialised policies all share one property — every customer wants a different answer. Why is "the right answer" so customer-specific in this section compared to, say, identity (where SAML+SCIM is the recommended default for almost everyone)?
  • The course has 8 learning outcomes spread across 18 lessons. Which outcome do you suspect produces the most customer-escalations after deployment, and why?
  • If a customer's compliance team mandates "log everything for 7 years" and their IT team budgets for 90 days of storage, what's the consultant's job in that workshop?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
Because each customer's risk tolerance and regulatory shape are different. SSL inspection on a financial-services customer is non-negotiable; on a healthcare customer it's deeply constrained by patient data law. Identity has a universal recommendation because the protocol is universally good; policy has no universal recommendation because the business context decides what's right.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
Timeout Policies (Authentication and Idle Connection). The default values almost never match what the customer actually wants — users get logged out mid-meeting, or sessions linger too long after a stolen laptop. It's not a security feature most engagements discuss at design time, but it's a user-experience feature every help desk hears about Week 1.
Q3 — MODEL ANSWER
Bring the two teams to the same conversation. Surface the actual numbers (storage cost per day per gigabyte, cost of a breach with missing logs) and let them negotiate against a shared framework. The DC's job isn't to pick — it's to make the trade-off visible enough that the customer can pick well. Suggest hybrid: shorter retention in Zscaler, long-tail archive in customer SIEM via NSS feeds.

💭THINK FIRST · POLICY DESIGN

Policy design starts with understanding the customer's intent — not their requested configuration. Customers often arrive with "we need to block these 200 sites"; the DC's job is to translate that into the right Zscaler policy structure, often with fewer, smarter rules.

  • Safemarch Corp wants to prevent users from accessing any website except specifically sanctioned sites. The course says this requires at minimum three policies. Why three? What does each one do?
  • ZPA has multiple policy types working in tandem. Why split them into multiple types rather than one unified "ZPA Policy" with every option in one place?
  • The course says policy is "an opportunity to help the organisation simplify their policies, possibly lowering the total number of policies by removing those that are unnecessary". Why is fewer-policies usually better, even if more policies seems more granular?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
(1) An SSL Inspection policy following the recommended baseline — without inspection you can't see the URL inside the HTTPS to make filtering decisions. (2) A URL Filtering policy with all categories selected and a Deny action — the "default deny" backstop. (3) A higher-ranked URL Filtering policy with an Allow action for the specific sanctioned URLs/categories — so the explicit allow-list fires before the deny-all. Order matters: most-specific allow rules above the deny-all.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
Separation of concerns. Access Policies decide "who can reach what", Timeout Policies decide "how long the session lives", Client Forwarding Policies decide "how it gets there", Inspection Policies decide "what gets looked at on the way". A single mega-policy would entangle these and make troubleshooting hopeless. Each policy type also runs at a different evaluation point in the request lifecycle — separating them by type makes the order explicit.
Q3 — MODEL ANSWER
Every policy is a permanent operational cost — someone has to remember it exists, test it during change windows, and understand it during incidents. Many small overlapping policies create silent drift; one well-thought-out policy is auditable. Customers often arrive with hundreds of legacy firewall rules; the cleanup opportunity is the consultant's chance to leave them better than they were found.
POLICY DESIGN

Why Policy Design matters — and how to approach it

Policy is at the heart of Zscaler and with that, you must make sure to start with configuring and enabling what will be the baseline policies of the Zscaler implementation. These are the general policies that will affect the greatest number of users across the organisation. More granular or fine-tuned policies can be created as a more comprehensive understanding of the business needs becomes clear.

Two starting principles:
· If the customer is replacing an existing solution, use this opportunity to help the organisation simplify their policies, possibly lowering the total number of policies by removing the ones that are unnecessary. There will be policies in use that should be in the Zscaler environment.
· It's common to find that new use up a policy in the previous solution is not how you would want to do it in Zscaler.
· As an example, a policy that was previously managed through a URL filtering rule to block a site would be better accomplished by using a Cloud Application Control Policy in Zscaler. Make sure that you understand what the intent of the policy is so that you can create the correct outcome in Zscaler.

ZPA Policy types

Policies in ZPA are the glue that binds applications and conditional access to their intended users. They are the bridge that connects users to applications while ensuring the criteria and security posture required by the business are met. There are four different types of policies in ZPA that work in tandem to provide seamless, secure, and criteria-based controlled access to applications for targeted users.

Access Policy
The primary "who gets to reach what" rule set. References identity + posture + application segments.
Timeout Policy
Authentication + Idle Connection — how long a user's session lasts before re-auth or disconnect.
Client Forwarding Policy
Whether ZCC forwards a user's traffic for a given app segment via ZPA or sends it elsewhere.
Specialised Policies
Browser Access, Double Encryption, Machine Authentication — narrower-scope policies for specific scenarios.

SCENARIO

1. Enable SSL inspection following the recommended policy (so the URL is visible inside HTTPS).

💭THINK FIRST · SSL INSPECTION

SSL inspection breaks the encryption between user and server so Zscaler can see, classify, and apply policy to traffic — then re-encrypts on the way out. It's the most powerful policy primitive in ZIA, and also the most likely to cause user-visible failures during rollout.

  • Why do most SSL inspection rollouts use a three-phase approach (exception list → IT department only → org-wide) rather than just enabling it globally on Day 1?
  • The predefined "Zscaler Recommended Exemptions" rule skips inspection of certain sites. Why does Zscaler ship pre-built exemptions rather than letting the customer build their own from scratch?
  • If a customer asks "should we inspect everything?", what's the consultant's reflexive question back?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
Because SSL inspection breaks any application that uses certificate pinning, mutual TLS, or proprietary TLS handshakes — and you can't predict in advance which apps in the customer's environment do this. A staged rollout lets the IT team identify and exempt these apps using their own usage patterns, before exposing the full user base to the risk. Day 1 global enablement means thousands of help-desk tickets simultaneously.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
Because Zscaler has telemetry across thousands of customers and knows which categories of sites (banking, government, certificate-pinned mobile apps, healthcare portals) reliably break under inspection — and either are sensitive enough that customers shouldn't be inspecting them anyway. The pre-built exemption list is institutional knowledge encoded as default config. The customer can still override, but starting from "Zscaler's exemptions" saves a year of trial and error.
Q3 — MODEL ANSWER
"What's your bypass strategy for cert-pinned and regulated traffic?" Because "inspect everything" without an exemption strategy creates broken apps within hours. The reflexive follow-up surfaces the operational plan; if the customer doesn't have one, the recommendation is the Zscaler-recommended baseline + IT review.
SSL INSPECTION

SSL Inspection at scale

SSL inspection lets Zscaler decrypt, classify, and policy-apply HTTPS traffic — making URL filtering, file-type control, sandboxing, and DLP work at all. Without inspection, most ZIA policy operates blind. But inspection is also where pilot deployments most often break, so it gets its own dedicated design discussion.

Three-phase rollout — the canonical approach

1
Phase 1 — Build the exception list
Include the technical team members from the project team and set up an exception list of categories that should not be running SSL Inspection on. Use the Zscaler Recommended Exemptions as the starting baseline.
2
Phase 2 — IT department only
Enable inspection scoped to the IT department first. They will hit application-pinning issues earliest and have the technical capability to identify, exempt, and document problem apps before broader rollout.
3
Phase 3 — Roll out to the rest of the organisation
With the exception list refined and IT-verified, enable inspection for the wider user base. Maintain the comms plan + escalation path during the cutover window.

Deployment scenarios — HTTPS Traffic from Known Locations vs Remote Users

i
WHERE THE TRAFFIC IS COMING FROM MATTERS
When an organisation's traffic is forwarded from a known location (an internet gateway configured in the Admin Portal), the Zscaler service applies URL Filtering and Cloud App Control policies as described below, depending on whether user authentication is enabled.
· Authentication Enabled: the service applies location-based policies, granular URL + Cloud App Control rules at the user level.
· Authentication Disabled: the service applies location-based URL + Cloud App Control policies. It cannot apply per-user policies because user authentication is disabled. Ensure that the appropriate location is specified in the Location setting of the URL and Cloud App Control policies.

💭THINK FIRST · LOGGING & SIEM

Logs are the audit trail of a Zscaler deployment. Storage is finite; logs grow continuously. The customer's choice of on-prem vs cloud SIEM, retention period, and log scope decides whether they can answer "what happened on this date last year" with confidence — or shrug.

  • The course gives a worked example: 500 users transacting at 1,000/hr for 100 hours per user generates ≈1.2 GB per hour, or 30 MB per 100 transactions. Why is storage planning such a load-bearing conversation at design time rather than something IT can "size later"?
  • What separates the right customer for an on-prem NSS from the right customer for a Cloud NSS?
  • If a customer's compliance requirement is "keep logs for 7 years", what's the realistic Zscaler architecture for that?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
Because the SIEM choice, the storage tier, and the network egress all need to be sized to match — and changing any of those mid-deployment is operationally invasive. Underestimate by 50% and the customer hits storage walls in Month 3; overestimate by 200% and Procurement asks why they paid for capacity that's empty. Design-time sizing is the difference between a smooth rollout and an emergency capacity-add four months in.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
On-prem NSS fits customers who already run their SIEM on a VM (Splunk, QELM, IBM QRadar, Securonix, ArcSight) and have free capacity for the NSS feed. Cloud NSS fits customers without on-prem SIEM infrastructure who want to subscribe to a Cloud-to-Cloud SIEM (Cloud NSS via APIs). The defining question: "where does your SIEM live today?" — answer that, and the choice picks itself.
Q3 — MODEL ANSWER
Hybrid retention. Keep ~30-90 days hot in Zscaler for fast queries and operational use, stream everything to the customer's SIEM (or a Cloud-to-Cloud archive via NSS), and rely on the customer's existing 7-year archival for compliance. Zscaler isn't a long-term archive; it's a real-time analytics platform with bridge connectors to the customer's archival system.
LOGGING & SIEM

Log storage, NSS feeds, and SIEM choice

Zscaler stores all logs of all transactions to and from the cloud — but Zscaler isn't designed to be the customer's long-term archive. Logs flow out to the customer's SIEM via Nanolog Streaming Service (NSS), which comes in two flavours: on-prem and cloud.

On-Premise vs Cloud-Based SIEM

On-Site NSS
For customers that host their SIEM on site, you will use a virtual machine (VM) as a means to stream the logs to the solution. NSS can be deployed on multiple platforms including vSphere, Amazon Web Services (AWS), or Azure, and requires minimal administration once setup.
Feeds output:
  • Web traffic = CSV, straight key/value, LogRhythm, Microsoft Cloud App Security, QRadar, RSA Security Analytics, Splunk, Symantec, or Custom
  • NSS for Firewall feeds can be formatted to output as CSV, JSON, Name Value Pairs, Tab-Separated, or Custom
Cloud-Based SIEM
An organisation can subscribe to use Cloud to Cloud log streaming (Cloud NSS). This means that instead of deploying an on-premises VM, you can configure a feed that will leverage Application Programming Interface (API) calls to push logs from the Zscaler Cloud Platform service into a log collection on the SIEM.
You may need to consider the Cloud NSS feeds for each log type: Web, Tunnel, Firewall, and SaaS Security. This is a fast growing method of deployment and more customers are embracing it.

Storage calculation — Safemarch worked example

The recommended amount of storage needed for the logs is 30 MB, but how do you calculate the storage needed?

i
SAFEMARCH ASSUMPTIONS
As an example, Safemarch Corp has 500 users, each user transacts at a rate of 1,000 transactions per hour, and the user is using the application for approximately 100 hours per user per work day. The size of a single transaction is approximately 30 MB, and the number of types of logs is approximately 3.
1,000 users × 100 transactions/hour × 4 Mbps × 5 types of logs = 1.2 GB per hour for 1000 users at 100 transactions per hour.
Storage Needed = 24 Hours × 365 Days × 10.5 TB (storage needed/year)
· Before we're finished, we have to account for the peaks and add some fudge factors.
· Fudge factors are added because the assumptions made are usually conservative, such as 24x7 work patterns at 4 Mbps, which is rare in business.
· Also, we are making an assumption of 100 transactions per user per hour, which can also be a varied operation. 30% seems to be the suitable fudge factor for what comes through.
Storage Needed/Year × Capacity for Spikes × Fudge Factor for Block Size Alignment = Storage Needed
10.5 TB × 1.3 (30% more capacity) × 1.3 (20% fudge factor) = 18.9 TB
Round up to 20 TB to be cautious. The calculations bring us to 20 TB of storage for logs for 1,000 users at 100 transactions per user per hour for a year.
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
NSS = Nanolog Streaming Service. Two flavours: On-Prem NSS (VM, on-site SIEM) and Cloud NSS (API push, Cloud-to-Cloud SIEM). Storage rule of thumb: ~1.2 GB/hr per 1,000 users at 100 tps. Always add 30% fudge factor + block-alignment buffer = roughly 1.7x raw. Compliance-grade retention belongs in the customer's SIEM, not Zscaler.
Analogy
Zscaler logs are like CCTV footage in a busy retail store. Every camera records continuously, and the security office (Zscaler) has enough storage for the last 90 days. For year-old footage you go to the off-site archive (the customer's SIEM). NSS is the courier service that moves the footage from the store to the archive — on-prem NSS is a van that drives to a local warehouse, Cloud NSS is a fibre-optic upload to a cloud archive. Same purpose, different vehicle.

💭THINK FIRST · TIMEOUT POLICIES

ZPA has two timeout policy types: Authentication Timeout and Idle Connection Timeout. They feel similar but solve different problems. Authentication = "when does the user have to log in again". Idle = "when does the connection get dropped because nothing's happening on it".

  • The Safemarch case study describes users hitting frequent timeout messages on a DFS filesystem, and the workaround being "log out and back into Zscaler Client Connector". What does this tell you about which timeout policy is misconfigured?
  • If a customer's application has a known long idle pattern (e.g. a finance app that polls every 4 hours), what's the correct way to handle it — and what's the wrong way?
  • The recommendations say "Timeout Policies can specify users and Segment Groups that the rule applies to". Why is per-segment-group scoping better than a single org-wide timeout?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
It's the Idle Connection Timeout that's misconfigured (too aggressive). The DFS filesystem session is going idle between user reads, the platform drops it, and the next read fails. Logging out and back in resets the session — a workaround, not a fix. The fix is a per-application-segment Idle Connection policy that increases the idle window for the DFS segment specifically.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
Correct: a Timeout Policy scoped to the Application Segment for that finance app, with an Idle Connection window longer than the polling interval (so a 4-hour idle would need 5-6 hours of headroom). Wrong: raising the org-wide default timeout to 5 hours, which leaves every other application's sessions open far longer than needed and weakens the security posture for everyone.
Q3 — MODEL ANSWER
Different applications have wildly different idle patterns and security profiles. A wildcard FQDN app might need long idles; a sensitive finance app should expire quickly. Per-segment scoping lets you tune each policy to its application's real behaviour. An org-wide timeout forces you to pick a value that's wrong for someone — either too short (UX pain) or too long (security risk).
TIMEOUT POLICIES

Authentication + Idle Connection Timeouts in ZPA

Authentication and Idle Connection Timeouts should be based on the sensitivity of the Application Segment. Apply frequent Authentication and Idle Timeouts on highly sensitive or third-party accessed applications.

Some applications will be negatively affected by an Authentication Timeout appearing to be a problem with the application. Use end-user feedback and the ZPA logs to understand if the Timeout Policies should be adjusted to satisfy application requirements.

Authentication vs Idle Connection — recommendations & considerations

Authentication Considerations
  • · Timeout Policies can specify users and Segment Groups that the rule applies to. These policies provide flexibility to enforce strict, short-term values for highly sensitive Application Segments or rights when providing long-term connectivity for applications that might need it
  • · When a user re-authenticates in any Application Segment, ZPA will get a new SAML assertion token causing the application timer to reset to start over
  • · If the IdP is defined as an application in a Client Forwarding policy, this means in turn the user will need a way to re-authenticate
Idle Connection Considerations
  • · If the IdP domain includes an application discovery policy, you must bypass the IdP to prevent authentication failures
  • · Because re-authentication settings are based on a Segment Group and not on the individual application, when a user is active in an application within that segment, considers that case to be active in all the segment

The Safemarch DFS scenario

!
USE CASE — IDLE CONNECTION TIMEOUTS
After the initial configuration of Wildcard FQDN access to private applications, Safemarch is reporting frequent issues with access to the DFS filesystems that the organisation uses. They are seeing several Timeout Policy messages when reviewing Diagnostics in the ZPA Admin Portal.

Safemarch reviewed the configuration of the Application Segments and Access Policies to confirm they are correct. Users are reporting that the way that they have been able to work around the issue is to log out and back into the Zscaler Client Connector.

Safemarch is requesting you, as the Deployment Specialist, to fix this issue and for an explanation for what they are observing in the logs. The fix: per-segment-group Idle Connection Timeout policy increase, scoped to the DFS application segment.
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
2 timeout types: Authentication (when re-login is required) + Idle Connection (when an idle session is dropped). Scope per Segment Group, not org-wide. Sensitive apps = short timeouts; long-polling apps = longer Idle Timeouts. "Log out and back in" workaround = your idle timeout is too aggressive for that segment.
Analogy
Timeout Policies are like the auto-lock on different rooms in a building. The board room (highly sensitive app) locks after 5 minutes of no movement. The break room (general intranet) locks after an hour. The night-cleaner's supply closet (long-polling background app) shouldn't lock at all during their shift. One blanket timer for every door is either annoyingly short for the cleaner or recklessly long for the board room.

💭THINK FIRST · SPECIALISED POLICIES

Four specialised policies sit alongside the core Access policy: Browser Access · Client Forwarding · Double Encryption · Machine Authentication. Each exists because one customer somewhere had a problem the core policy alone couldn't solve.

  • Client Forwarding Policy has three actions: Forward, Only Forward Allowed Applications, and Bypass. When would a consultant recommend Bypass for an application segment — and what's the security tradeoff?
  • Double Encryption is recommended when a customer has "an internal web app that does not use SSL" in a geopolitically sensitive location. Why is wrapping non-SSL in Zscaler's tunnel not sufficient by itself — what extra encryption are we adding?
  • Machine Authentication uses Machine Groups + Machine Provisioning Keys + Machine Tunnels so Windows can connect to ZPA before the user logs in. What customer scenario actually requires pre-login ZPA — and why not just have users log in faster?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
When the application is internet-facing and doesn't benefit from ZPA brokerage — e.g. a public SaaS service the customer wants users to reach directly. The tradeoff is that you lose ZPA's identity-aware enforcement and posture checks for that segment. Use Bypass sparingly and document each instance; it's a permission slip to opt out of zero-trust for a specific app, and every one weakens the overall posture by exactly that amount.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
Zscaler's outer tunnel encrypts traffic between the user device and the App Connector. But once inside the customer's data centre, the App Connector talks to the web app — and that hop is plaintext if the web app doesn't use SSL. Double Encryption wraps a second TLS layer that extends end-to-end through the App Connector to the application server (or as close as the app supports). In sensitive locations, plaintext inside the data centre is still a leakage risk.
Q3 — MODEL ANSWER
Active Directory password changes at the CTRL+ALT+DEL screen — before the user is logged in. The machine needs to talk to a domain controller to validate the new password, but there's no user session yet for ZPA to authenticate. Machine Tunnels let the machine itself authenticate to ZPA pre-login so the AD password-change path works. Without it, customers with strict AD password policies have a permanent help-desk loop.
SPECIALISED POLICIES

Browser Access · Client Forwarding · Double Encryption · Machine Authentication

The specialised ZPA policies all have their own deployment patterns and use cases. With each deployment, these policies will become more specific to the use case and deployment need but will be consistent in "how" you should implement each.

Client Forwarding Policy

i
OVERVIEW
Client Forwarding Policy is commonly used but addresses specific circumstances. As a Deployment Specialist, you need to understand it well in order to use it as a solution. Client Forwarding Policy has 3 possible Actions:
· Forward to ZPA: by default, Client Forwarding Policy is configured so that the Zscaler Client Connector receives all matched traffic for the Application Segments
· Only Forward Allowed Applications: if a rule set to "Only Forward Allowed Applications" is applied to the user, the Zscaler Client Connector receives the list of applications that the user is permitted to access. This bypass action sends traffic for unauthenticated applications across the segment that is in the Application Segment
· Bypass ZPA: the action is the opposite of "Forward to ZPA", allowing specific traffic to bypass ZPA. This bypass action is more flexible compared to configuring bypasses in the Application Segment

Double Encryption — Safemarch use case

!
USE CASE
Your customer Safemarch operates offices in various regions in the world. In a specific geolocation (Eastern Europe), there is a threat of war breaking out. Safemarch has an internal web application that does not use SSL. In order to ensure that the private application's data remains secure, Safemarch wants to know how they could encrypt the traffic.

Solution: This is a classic use case that calls for Double Encryption. Double Encryption will ensure that the web traffic stays within an encrypted tunnel from the user device to the App Connector. Here's how to configure double encryption:
1. Follow the process for certification requirements and upload the certificates to the Administration → Certificates section of the ZPA Admin Portal
2. Ensure that the Zscaler Client Connector and the App Connectors use these certificates
3. The Application Segment has the Double Encryption option set to "Enabled"

Machine Authentication

i
OVERVIEW
Leveraging the combination of Machine Groups, Machine Provisioning Keys, and Machine Tunnels, you can allow a user's Windows device to establish a connection to ZPA before logging into the Zscaler Client Connector, or even before Windows login.
· Machine groups are a set of internal, local machines that need to connect to a ZPA application, such as Active Directory
· Machine Provisioning Keys are used as an ID to the machine and enable the ZPA Cloud to verify the machine's authenticity
· Machine Tunnels are configured from the Zscaler Client Connector

Recommendation: Machine Tunnels should be used in environments that use Active Directory joined devices, and users typically change passwords at the CTRL+ALT+DEL screen.

Browser Access

Browser Access lets users reach private web applications without installing the Zscaler Client Connector — useful for unmanaged devices, contractors, and BYOD scenarios. Pair with strict MFA at the IdP and per-application access policies to maintain posture without the agent.

Each one exists to unblock a specific customer scenario the core Access policy can't handle alone.

PRIVATE SERVICE EDGE

Deploying a ZIA Private Service Edge

Private Service Edges (PSEs) are dedicated ZIA edge nodes deployed inside the customer's environment — for customers who need sovereignty, performance, or latency guarantees the public Zscaler edge can't promise. PSE deployment follows an 8-step process.

1
Engage Zscaler Support and Sizing
Open a support ticket to engage the Private Service Edge team. Provide expected throughput, location, redundancy targets, and use-case context so Zscaler can size correctly.
2
Site Preparation
Confirm rack space, power, cooling, network ports, and IP address allocation at the deployment site. Coordinate with the customer's data centre team.
3
Install Private Service Edge
Upon receipt of the Private Service Edges from Zscaler, install them at the prepared site, cable per the topology, and power on.
4
Initial Configuration
Configure management IP, DNS, NTP, and SSH access. Validate basic connectivity to the Zscaler control plane.
5
Cluster Registration
Register the PSEs with the customer's ZIA cloud tenant via the Admin Portal. Group into a cluster for HA where appropriate.
6
Policy & Cloud Sync
Once registered, the PSE pulls policy and configuration from the central ZIA platform. Validate that the synced policy matches the customer's intended scope.
7
Validate & Cut Over
Direct a test population of users through the PSE, validate inspection and logging, then expand to broader user base via traffic forwarding rules.
8
Self-Provisioning of GRE Tunnels (optional)
If your organisation uses GRE tunnels to forward traffic to the Zscaler service, follow the standard procedure for Self-Provisioning of GRE Tunnels. Private Service Edge VIP addresses are available for selection on the GRE Tunnels page in the ZIA Admin Portal.
Note: Self-provisioning of GRE tunnels towards Private Service Edge clusters is only supported for clusters deployed on public IP space (i.e. a non-NAT environment). If you need to build a GRE tunnel towards a Private Service Edge cluster deployed within a NAT environment, contact Zscaler Support or submit a Zscaler Support ticket.
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
PSE = customer-hosted ZIA edge for sovereignty / latency / throughput reasons. 8-step deployment: Support engagement → Site prep → Install → Initial config → Cluster register → Policy sync → Validate & cut over → Optional GRE self-provisioning. Self-provisioned GRE is public IP only; NAT environments need a support ticket.
Analogy
A Private Service Edge is a customer-installed franchise branch of Zscaler. The brand, policies, menu, and uniforms (configuration + policies) come from the franchisor (Zscaler) — but the rent, lights, and local staff (rack space, power, cabling) are the franchisee's. The customer gets a Zscaler edge with their own keys; Zscaler gets to keep the policy and update process consistent across every franchise globally.
