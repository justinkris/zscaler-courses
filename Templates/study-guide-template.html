OVERVIEW

What is the Zscaler Deployment Methodology?

The Deployment Methodology is the structured framework Zscaler Professional Services uses to take a customer from signed SoW to a fully operational, customer-owned deployment. It exists to ensure consistent, low-risk engagements and to protect both the customer and the PS team from scope creep, surprise blockers, and failed go-lives.

It comprises six sequential phases, each with a specific purpose, set of deliverables, and an implicit exit gate. You do not move to the next phase without completing the current one — and any request to add scope mid-phase triggers the Change Request process.

Central insight: Every phase has an exit gate. The SoW and the HLD are the two contracts that hold the project together — one commercial, one technical. Touch either and you re-baseline the project.
THE 6 PHASES

Initiation → Plan → Configure → Pilot → Production → Transition

1
Initiation WHO
Triggered the moment the SoW is signed. This is the handover from sales to PS. It is not about requirements — it is about getting the project off the ground administratively.

Deliverables: Kickoff meeting · Stakeholder map / RACI · Project charter (scope, timeline, comms cadence) · PS team assignment · Tenant access logistics confirmed.
2
Plan WHAT
Deep-dive design phase. This is where you run design workshops, fill out the IDA, and produce the High-Level Design document. Everything you're going to build is defined here.

Deliverables: IDA completed (ZIA + ZPA) · Traffic forwarding method confirmed · IdP/SAML metadata exchanged · App Connector placement agreed · Cloud/tenant selection confirmed · HLD drafted and signed off by customer sponsor.
3
Configure BUILD
Build the tenant per the signed-off HLD. No users are live yet. Any request to add scope here triggers a formal Change Request.

Deliverables: ZIA / ZPA / ZDX configured · SSL Inspection enabled · Policies built · App Segments and Connector Groups created · SAML IdP integration live · Tenant tested and ready for pilot users.
4
Pilot Roll-out PROVE
Validate the deployment with a representative subset of users before committing the full organisation. A poorly designed pilot gives false confidence. See the Pilot Design section.

Deliverables: Pilot group live · Issues logged and resolved · Exit criteria met (see below) · Pilot sign-off from customer technical lead.
5
Production Roll-out SCALE
Expand from the pilot group to the full user population. Roll out in waves — don't flip all 5,000 users at once.

Deliverables: All users onboarded · All sites live · Tunnel capacity confirmed at full load · Helpdesk ticket volume tracking normal.
6
Transition HAND OVER
Final phase. Review all work done, provide short and long-term recommendations, resolve outstanding items, and transfer ownership to the customer's ops team.

Deliverables: Runbook / operations guide · Admin training completed · Short and long-term recommendations documented · Support handover (PS → TAM / support) · Project formally closed.
!
EXAM TRAP — INITIATION VS PLAN
The exam will describe a scenario where the SoW has just been signed and ask what you do next. The wrong answer is "gather requirements" — that's Plan. The right answer is "run the kickoff, establish the RACI, assign the PS team" — that's Initiation. The two phases have a hard boundary: Initiation = who + how we work. Plan = what we're building.

PILOT DESIGN & EXIT CRITERIA

Why pilot design matters — and what a good one looks like

A poorly designed pilot gives the sponsor false confidence. The most common mistake is using IT users only — they are unrepresentative on every dimension that matters.

Why IT-only pilots fail

RiskIT UsersGeneral Users
App coverageUse a small set of IT tools — App Segments and policies only proven for their appsUse the full breadth of departmental applications — gaps surface immediately
Issue reportingSelf-heal silently — misconfiguration never surfacesRaise helpdesk tickets — issues become visible
Capacity50 users at HQ doesn't test branch tunnels or App Connector loadRepresentative load across all sites stresses real-world capacity
BehaviourTechnically compliant — won't trigger edge-case DLP or URL filtering rulesNormal browsing and file behaviour — exercises full policy surface

Well-designed pilot matrix

  • Power users from each department — cover application breadth. If Finance works, all Finance users work.
  • Users from each site / location — prove tunnel capacity and connectivity at every egress point, not just HQ.
  • All device types in scope — Windows, Mac, mobile if applicable. Don't pilot Windows-only then deploy to 500 Mac users.
  • Users who have been told they are on the pilot — they know to report issues. Silent pilots produce silent failures.
⟳
THE TASTE TESTER ANALOGY
Using only IT users to pilot Zscaler is like asking the head chef to taste-test a dish before it goes to paying customers. The chef knows the ingredients, can work around the flaws, and won't complain the same way a customer would. You need real diners — people who just want it to work — to know whether the dish is actually ready.

Pilot exit criteria — the full list

Zero helpdesk tickets is not an exit criterion. It might just mean nobody noticed. Here is what you actually need before recommending production go-live:

CategoryExit criterion
InfrastructureThroughput baseline confirmed — ideally a 7-day graph covering peak load. No tunnel drops or failover events.
InfrastructureApp Connector capacity confirmed — no CPU/memory alerts, connection queue healthy.
InfrastructureDevice capability confirmed for chosen forwarding method (GRE/IPSec/ZCC) at each site.
PolicyAll pilot users' applications resolving correctly — zero access denials from missing App Segments or policy gaps.
PolicySSL Inspection, URL Filtering, and DLP (if in scope) behaving as expected — no false positives blocking legitimate work.
IdentityAll pilot users authenticating successfully via SAML. No clock skew, cert, or attribute errors in logs.
IdentitySCIM provisioning confirmed — all pilot users and groups appearing correctly in the tenant.
ProcessHelpdesk ticket volume trending down to near-zero and stable.
ProcessAll issues raised during the pilot have been resolved and documented.
ProcessFormal sign-off obtained from pilot users and customer technical lead — not just "no news is good news."
CHANGE CONTROL

What to do when a customer asks to add scope mid-project

This scenario appears in the exam because it tests whether you understand the purpose of the phases — not just what they're called. The wrong answer is to just start building. The right answer follows this sequence every time.

1
Understand the use case — ask what problem they're trying to solve before touching anything. The feature they asked for may not be the right solution, or it may already be covered by current scope.
2
Assess the impact — scope (does it change the HLD?), timeline (does the pilot date slip?), effort (extra Configure + Pilot work), licensing (do they own the SKU?).
3
Raise a Change Request / Change Order — the formal mechanism against the SoW. Involves PS lead, PM, customer sponsor, and sometimes the account team.
4
Document the decision — approved, deferred, or rejected. Either way it goes into the project log. Silence is not a decision.
!
WATCH OUT — "SURE, WE'LL ADD IT"
The exam wrong answer is agreeing to add scope without a formal Change Request. This is how projects miss go-live dates. Scope creep during Configure is one of the top reasons Zscaler deployments slip. Always name the CR process explicitly — saying "we'd have a chat" is insufficient.
★
YOUR SESSION GAP — MEMORISE THIS
In the session quiz you correctly identified the process steps but didn't name the formal mechanism. On the exam: the answer is always "raise a Change Request / Change Order against the SoW." Use that exact language.
RACI FRAMEWORK

Who does what in a ZDDC engagement?

RACI eliminates ambiguity in multi-party projects. In a complex ZIA + ZPA deployment, multiple organisations and teams are involved. Without RACI, escalation paths break down and decisions stall. The RACI document is produced in the Initiation phase as a formal output.

Letter Meaning Typical role in ZDDC Communication
R Responsible — does the work PS Technical Lead — builds the config, runs workshops, delivers the solution Two-way
A Accountable — owns the outcome (one only) Project Manager — answers for the project outcome Two-way
C Consulted — input before decisions Customer Network/Security team + PS SMEs — firewall rules, IdP details Two-way
I Informed — kept updated on progress Customer executives — need visibility without being in the weeds One-way
!
R vs A — THE EXAM TRAP
Responsible = does the work. Accountable = owns the outcome. The PS consultant is Responsible. The Project Manager is Accountable. There can be multiple Responsible parties — but only one Accountable person per task.
TRANSITION DELIVERABLES

What do you hand over at the end?

The Transition phase is the formal close of the PS engagement. Four key outputs must be delivered before the customer's team can operate independently.

Runbook
Operational SOPs for the customer's team post-handover. Answers "how do we do X day-to-day?" — adding a new location, whitelisting a cloud app, creating a URL filtering rule. This is the operations manual.
As-Built Document
A record of what was actually configured and — critically — why each decision was made. Includes not just the what (SSL bypass for banking.com) but the why (certificate pinning causes app breakage). This context prevents the customer's team from accidentally breaking things months later.
Short-Term Recommendations
Quick wins and immediate actions for after handover. Often items that were out of scope but low-effort — e.g. "enable DNS Security on remaining locations within 30 days."
Long-Term Recommendations
Strategic roadmap for continued maturity. Features to adopt next, architecture improvements, expansion plans.
→
EXAM TIP — MAP THE QUESTION
"How do we add a new location next year?" → Runbook. "Why did you bypass SSL for that domain?" → As-Built. "What should we do in the next 90 days?" → Short-term recommendations.
ANALOGIES

Ways to think about it

⟳
BUILDING A HOUSE
Initiation = sign the contract, agree what you're building, define RACI. Plan = draw the blueprints. Configure = build the structure. Pilot = invite 5 people to test-live and report problems. Production = everyone moves in. Transition = hand over the keys, the manual, and a list of what to improve next year.
⟳
RACI AS AN ER TEAM
The surgeon (R) does the operation. The attending physician (A) is accountable for the patient outcome. The specialist you call for input (C) is consulted. The patient's family (I) is informed. Only one person is accountable — the attending. Multiple surgeons can be responsible for different parts. Nobody asks the family to make surgical decisions, but they deserve to know what's happening.
EXAM TIPS

What the exam tests for this domain

→
PHASE BOUNDARY QUESTIONS
The exam loves the Initiation/Plan boundary and the Configure/Pilot boundary. When you see "SoW just signed" → Initiation. When you see "scope change requested during Configure" → Change Request. When you see "pilot showed zero issues" → check the pilot design before recommending go-live.
→
TRANSITION IS MORE THAN DOCS
Don't reduce Transition to "hand over documentation." The exam expects: short and long-term recommendations, path to resolution of outstanding items, and a clear explanation of how Zscaler will continue supporting the customer's transformation. Ops guide + training + recommendations + formal close.
→
CHINA PRESENCE — PLAN PHASE FLAG
China users trigger China Premium Access — a specific Zscaler add-on. This affects cloud selection and must be confirmed during the Plan phase (or ideally pre-sale). Changing this post-provisioning is disruptive. If you see China in a scenario, flag this immediately in your answer.
  • The IDA (Initial Deployment Assessment) is a Plan phase tool — not Initiation.
  • HLD sign-off is the exit gate for the Plan phase. Don't move to Configure without it.
  • Zscaler's cloud infrastructure does not get overwhelmed by 5,000 users. App Connectors, IdP, and branch tunnels do.
  • GRE is the preferred tunnel method — higher soft limit than IPSec. Confirm GRE capability before defaulting to IPSec.
  • ZPA LSS (log streaming to SIEM) requires a separate licence. Always confirm before designing SIEM integration into ZPA.
