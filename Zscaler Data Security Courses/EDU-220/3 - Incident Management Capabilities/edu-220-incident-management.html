Incident Management

Notification options for end users and admins, Zscaler Workflow Automation, DLP incident management, and the final knowledge check.

Identify, assess, and respond to security incidents and service disruptions swiftly.

Zscaler Workflow Automation streamlines data protection workflows by automating incident management — enabling quick remediation of DLP and CASB incidents across the organisation.

🤔BEFORE YOU READ — THINK
Every security operations team faces the same paradox: the more alerts you generate, the less attention each one receives. Incident management isn't really about detecting incidents — it's about deciding which ones deserve a human's finite attention.
  • If your DLP tool produces 500 alerts per day and your team can review 50, what happens to the other 450 — and is "missing" them better or worse than not detecting them at all?
  • When does asking the end user to justify their own violation save admin time, and when does it just shift the problem somewhere less visible?
  • What's the difference between an "incident" and a "ticket" — and why does that distinction matter for how you measure your team's effectiveness?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
They sit in a backlog and quietly age. Practically, "alerts you cannot review" function as "no detection" from a risk standpoint — except they also create legal exposure ("you generated the alert and did nothing") and operational drag. It is actively worse than not detecting them, which is why the goal of incident management is not "more alerts" but "fewer-better-higher-fidelity" alerts: tuning, grouping, deduplication, and end-user self-service to keep the queue inside human capacity.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
End-user justification works when the action is ambiguous — soft block on a personal upload, print of a flagged document — where the user holds the context the admin lacks. It saves admin time and creates an audit trail. It shifts the problem when admins stop reviewing the justifications: "I needed it for a project" becomes a magic password, and the queue moves invisibly back to the unreviewed pile. Justification is only useful with periodic spot-review.
Q3 — MODEL ANSWER
An incident is a policy event — a specific DLP violation, an exposure, a behaviour anomaly. A ticket is a work item assigned to a human to investigate or remediate. Conflating them creates the wrong metric: counting tickets created looks like activity but tells you nothing about risk reduction. Measuring incidents resolved (and their MTTD/MTTR) tells you whether the team is keeping up. Tickets are administration; incidents are the work.
WHAT IS INCIDENT MANAGEMENT?

Identifying, analysing, and resolving incidents

Incident Management is a process of identifying, analysing, and resolving critical incidents which could lead to issues in an organisation. It is a critical component of any security framework — helping organisations mitigate the impact of security incidents, minimise downtime, and prevent future occurrences.

Incidents can involve:

  • Security threats (e.g., malware, phishing attempts)
  • Network or application failures
  • Unusual or suspicious activity flagged for investigation
In data security context: When adopting DLP and CASB (SaaS Security API) capabilities, alerts are generated that require administrator teams to investigate and respond. Zscaler provides tools to manage these alerts through multiple channels — delegating violations to end users where appropriate.
ANALOGY
Incident management is the emergency room triage nurse — not the doctor. Her job isn't to cure anyone; it's to decide, in seconds, who bleeds out without attention and who can safely wait. A DLP platform without triage is an ER where every cut and every gunshot wound get the same waiting-room ticket.

ASK A QUESTION
MY NOTES
🤔BEFORE YOU READ — THINK
Choosing how to notify a user isn't a UI decision — it's a behavioural one. The channel shapes whether the user pauses to think, clicks through reflexively, or ignores the message entirely. The "best" notification depends on the outcome you want, not the technology that's available.
  • If a browser-based block shows up mid-upload, will the user actually read the justification box, or paste anything to get past the screen?
  • Why might a Slack notification get a more honest justification than an SMTP email that arrives 20 minutes later?
  • What's the cost of routing every admin notification through SIEM, ServiceNow, AND email — and at what point does redundancy become alert fatigue?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
In practice, most users skim and reflexively paste — especially under deadline pressure. The browser block has to do design work: clear policy statement, required justification field with a minimum length, contextual reason ("This file contains 12 credit card numbers"), and a visible audit notice. Without those, the block becomes a speed bump and the data still goes where the user wanted it. Block UX is policy enforcement; bad UX is theatre.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
Latency and context. Slack notifications arrive in seconds, in the channel where the user is already working, with the incident fresh in mind. Email arrives 20 minutes later — by then the user has moved on, the context is gone, and the message either becomes "what was this about?" or gets archived unread. Honest justifications happen when the user remembers what they were doing, which is right now, not later.
Q3 — MODEL ANSWER
Triple-routing has real cost: it triples the surfaces to monitor, multiplies the chance of inconsistent acknowledgement, and is the classic recipe for alert fatigue (everyone sees the alert; nobody acts because someone else must be). The right pattern is one system of record (SIEM or ServiceNow) and one human-attention channel (Slack/Teams) for urgent action. Email is documentation, not alert. Redundancy becomes fatigue the moment ownership becomes ambiguous.
NOTIFICATION METHODS

How Zscaler notifies users and admins

The first priority in incident management is enabling end users to self-serve on violations — delegating responsibility and reducing admin burden. Zscaler provides several notification channels that can be customised with your organisation's logo and messaging.

End-User Notification Options

🌐
Browser-Based Notification
Customisable page shown when a user attempts a blocked action. Can block or allow the transaction while notifying the user. Supports form POST for justification feedback back to the admin.
💬
Slack / Teams Connector
Notifies users via Slack or Microsoft Teams when a violation is detected. Users can provide justification directly within the chat platform — many organisations prefer this over browser notifications.
🖥️
Zscaler Client Connector Pop-Up
A pop-up on the endpoint via the Zscaler Client Connector — even for endpoint DLP violations. Same idea: block or allow, ask for justification, or coach the user not to repeat the violation.

Administrator Notification and Integration Options

  • Email notifications — SMTP-based alerts sent to DLP admins when incidents are triggered
  • Secure ICAP protocol — for integration with on-premises systems
  • SIEM integration — real-time log streaming to Splunk, QRadar, and other SIEM tools
  • ServiceNow integration — automated ticket creation for incident tracking
  • On-premises incident receiver — for archiving incidents locally
★
KEY ANSWER
When asked how Zscaler notifies users about policy violations: the answer is browser-based notifications, Slack/Teams connectors, or the Zscaler Client Connector — NOT by sending personal emails, shutting down systems, or requiring calls to IT support.
ANALOGY
A browser block is a speed bump — it forces a moment of friction at the exact place the user was going. A Slack ping is a tap on the shoulder afterward — quieter, but easier to dismiss. An SMTP email to the admin is a letter in the mail — accurate, slow, and almost never read first.

ASK A QUESTION
MY NOTES
🤔BEFORE YOU READ — THINK
Automation is seductive because it scales — but every automated decision is one a human used to make, and every shortcut hides a judgment call. Workflow Automation isn't replacing your incident analysts; it's encoding the rules they already follow. The danger is encoding rules they only thought they followed.
  • If WFA auto-groups incidents by user and policy, what kinds of patterns might it hide that a human reviewing each alert in isolation would catch?
  • When you escalate to a manager automatically, are you accelerating resolution — or just diffusing accountability up the org chart?
  • What does "Time to Triage" tell you that "Time to Resolve" doesn't, and which one should the team actually be optimising for?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
Cross-user patterns. Auto-grouping by user surfaces one person's behaviour clearly but hides coordinated activity — five users in finance all uploading to the same external domain at the same hour. A human reviewing alerts in isolation might spot the cluster; grouped views by user alone will not. The fix is multiple lenses (by user, by destination, by policy, by time window) — automation should make alternate views easier, not collapse them.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
Both. Auto-escalation accelerates resolution when the manager has the context and authority to act faster than the analyst. It diffuses accountability when escalation becomes a reflex — every ambiguous alert gets sent up, the manager is overloaded, and nobody actually owns the decision. The cure is escalation criteria that are specific (severity, asset class, dwell time) rather than "when in doubt" — making escalation a deliberate action, not a default.
Q3 — MODEL ANSWER
Time to Triage measures how quickly the team acknowledges and assigns an incident; Time to Resolve measures how quickly it closes. Optimising only for Resolve hides backlog growth — incidents may close fast but newer ones sit untouched for hours. Triage time is the leading indicator: it tells you whether new alerts are getting human eyes promptly, before they age. Both matter, but Triage is where queue health shows up first.
ZSCALER WORKFLOW AUTOMATION

Automated, closed-loop incident management

Zscaler Workflow Automation (WFA) streamlines data security workflows by automating incident management — enabling quick remediation of data protection incidents. It automatically groups incidents based on various attributes and provides a complete audit trail.

WFA integrates with:

  • Zscaler Internet Access (ZIA) — captures Data Protection incidents generated from DLP policies
  • Zscaler Digital Experience (ZDX) — generates enriched tickets for different events triggered by data defined in ZIA
  • Business Insights — manages usage of SaaS application licences provisioned to end users

Key Features

Incident Grouping
Automatically groups incidents into prioritised incident groups based on the originating user, the user's manager, and the triggering activity or policy. Groups can be assigned to different admins for parallel handling.
Workflow Management Tools
Notifies users for incident justification or escalates incidents to managers for approval. Admins can view the triggering content, apply remediation notes, and track the full lifecycle of each incident.
Automated Workflows
Manages and remediates DLP incidents efficiently — escalating violations to relevant personnel, automating ticket creation, and resolving incidents with a full audit trail of every action taken.

✓
INCIDENT LIFECYCLE
1. Incident triggered → admin reviews policy and triggering content.
2. Admin notifies end user (email/Slack/browser) requesting justification.
3. User submits justification → admin reviews feedback.
4. If escalated → manager/approver receives escalation with higher detail.
5. Manager can accept, escalate further, or deny.
6. Resolution recorded → count updated → incident closed.
ANALOGY
Workflow Automation is a factory conveyor belt for incidents. Raw alerts go in one end, get sorted into bins (incident groups), routed to the right station (admin, user, manager), and roll out the other end stamped "resolved" with a full paper trail. Without it, you have the same parts — but every worker is sprinting between piles on the floor.

ASK A QUESTION
MY NOTES
🤔BEFORE YOU ANSWER — THINK
The wrong answers on a knowledge check aren't random — they're the assumptions an untrained admin would actually make. Before you pick, work out why each wrong option feels plausible. That's where the real learning happens.
  • Why is "send a personal email" a tempting but flawed answer — what does it confuse about who Zscaler actually integrates with?
  • What would "automatically shut down the user's system" cost the business compared to a soft block with justification?
  • If the right answer is three channels (browser, Slack/Teams, Client Connector), what does that tell you about Zscaler's philosophy on user experience vs. enforcement?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
It confuses one-off ad-hoc communication with platform-level integration. Zscaler does not "send a personal email" — it integrates with email infrastructure (SMTP relays), collaboration platforms (Slack, Teams), and endpoint UX (Client Connector notifications). The distractor is plausible because "email the user" sounds like a notification; the right answer is structured channels that scale, are auditable, and respect the same security boundaries as the platform itself.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
Shutting down a user's system is hours of lost work, a help-desk ticket, possibly an executive complaint, and zero new information about whether the user actually intended harm. A soft block with justification costs the user thirty seconds, surfaces intent through the justification text, and keeps the team productive. The proportionate response always wins unless the action is unambiguously catastrophic — at which point a hard block (not a system shutdown) is the right tool.
Q3 — MODEL ANSWER
Three channels mean Zscaler reaches the user in whatever context they are working — browser for web actions, Slack/Teams for chat-driven workflows, Client Connector for OS-level events. The philosophy: notify the user where the action happened, in the channel they are already attending to. Enforcement remains strict; user experience is preserved by meeting people in their workflow rather than dragging them out of it. Block what must be blocked, but communicate humanely.
